This is a *group* assignment! Feel free to work in groups of up to 3 individuals.

Having done such good work on Solicitorbot, you have been promoted to an advertising data scientist at Forney Industries, which is just about to launch its newest Home Automation / Defense line of robotics.

The beta phase of Killbot 3000 Defendotron 3001 is complete, and initial sales have been made to a variety of customers around the US.

After each of these initial sales pitches (that ended in purchases or otherwise), Forney Industries collected demographic data on each participant, and quizzed them on a variety of metrics that were believed to be in some way related to the outcome of the sale.

The launch of the Defendotron line is now upon us, and two customizable models are available for public consumption:

  • Defendotron Basic [$500]: comes with a siren and tazer to alert you and protect your home from intruders.

  • Defendotron 'Murica [$1,776]: comes with a full arsenal, including SAM missile array to protect from drone attack. Plays the Star Spangled Banner in place of siren when an intruder is detected.


The Data

Pending the launch of the Defendotron line, you are now analyzing the data collected by the in-person sales team in which a variety of traits and features must be integrated into an online advertising agent meant to select an ad that is most likely to be converted into a sale.

In particular, before giving their pitch, the members of the sales team showed participants ads with two key variables:

  • Ad Video Choice [Ad1]: the video shows either a scare tactic of a home robbery (Ad1 = 0) or a patriotic montage with lots of guns and flags waving (Ad1 = 1).

  • Ad Music Choice [Ad2]: the video either has a narrator's voice-over explaining the product (Ad2 = 0) or some tasteful classic rock that mentions freedom a bunch (Ad2 = 1).

The sales team showed each of these ad combinations at random, then collected data on several important, binary features (described below) as well as the *ternary* (3 values) outcome of the sales call:

  • Political Leaning [P]: the political leaning of the interviewee (0 = conservative, 1 = liberal)

  • Age Group [A]: generational age group of interviewee (0 = millennial, 1 = baby boomer+)

  • Position on Gun Control [G]: whether or not the interviewee is in support of stricter gun control legislation (0 = no, 1 = yes)

  • Possession of Home/Renter's Insurance [I]: whether or not the interviewee possesses home or renter's insurance (0 = no, 1 = yes)

  • Perceived Threat of Crime [T]: whether or not the interviewee believes they live in a crime-filled neighborhood (0 = no, 1 = yes)

  • Perceived Threat of Foreign Invasion [F]: whether or not the interviewee believes in a foreign threat to the homeland (0 = no, 1 = yes)

  • Home Ownership [H]: whether or not the interviewee owns their home (0 = doesn't own, 1 = owns).

  • Sold Defendotron [S]: outcome of the sales pitch (0 = didn't buy anything, 1 = bought Defendotron 'Murica, 2 = bought Defendotron basic)

You can find the dataset they collected from thousands of participants in the project skeleton.


Your Task

Your task: create a decision-network-based Ad Agent that will maximize Forney Industries' sales of Defendotrons online based on any of the user's collected demographic data on the metrics above. The agent must select the combination of Ad characteristics [Ad1, Ad2] that maximizes the expected utility of a sale.

The problem: for each online sale, only some of these features may be known / observed, and so we need a means of deciding which ad combination to show them to maximize our expected sales.

Specifically, you are tasked with the following:

  • Gain an understanding of the relationship between the variables collected by the sales team.

  • Use this understanding to create an autonomous advertising agent.

Bayesian Network Modeling

Since we are starting this endeavor with only the dataset, our first task is to examine the putative structure of the BN using a bottom-up approach.

This is not difficult to automate for some models, but in general, may require some companion human intervention. At the very least, it gives us and our greater marketing team some insights into what our customer-base looks like.

However, when it comes to using Bayesian Networks to model causal relationships, the story is a bit trickier for a familiar reason:

Observational equivalence: we may have independence relationships between variables that can be explained by different structures (e.g., \(X \rightarrow Z \rightarrow Y\) and \(X \leftarrow Z \rightarrow Y\) make the same independence claim that \(X \indep Y~|~Z\), but which is the "correct" causal story requires a human's assumptions since this is not a determination that can be made from the data alone).

As such, we'll use a structure learning tool that can help us determine what parts of the BN structure can be learned from data and which require humans to set into place.


There are several popular tools for BN structure learning, but one of the most developed and used in academic settings is Tetrad, which you can find and download below (just get the most recent version and download the associated *-launch.jar):

Download Tetrad

Tetrad is developed at CMU/Pitt and is useful in both probabilistic and causal structure discovery -- it does not provide tools for inference, which we will find elsewhere once we have determined the BN structure.

What follows is a walkthrough for using Tetrad to determine the BN structure modeling the dataset given above.


  1. Download the latest version of Tetrad (instructions above)

  2. Launch Tetrad (can just double click on the .jar file though Unix systems might need the terminal), after which you will be greeted with a new project window. Tetrad is modular, allowing you to make an analysis project that is comprised of various linkable stages.

  3. Since we are interested in learning a network structure from data, go to the menu at the top and select Pipelines -> Search then Estimate. What appears is a pipeline that will find as much of the network structure as possible, have us decide on the final model, and then will estimate the network parameters (CPTs). To do this, double click on each module in the following order, and perform the given steps:

    1. Data: load the dataset into Tetrad via File -> Load Data, making sure to select "Discrete" data in the dialogue that pops up. You should see all of the data in the table after this step.

    2. Search: we'll now search for the network structure. In this module, you'll see that there are *many* structure learning algorithms for a variety of scenarios.

      • In our case, we'll use the PC Variants method, which you shall select, then click "Set parameters" and finally "Run and Generate Graph." (there's also a small snippet on the right with what it does -- neat!)

      • You'll notice that the structure is *almost* completely directed, except for some edges that are not. These are the edges that are not discernable from the data alone and comprise the models in the observational equivalence class of the data.

    3. Graph: it's time for us to provide the direction of the edges that had an unknown orientation from the structure learning.

      • Select "Choose Dag in Pattern;" the edges that need to have an orientation chosen will be highlighted in yellow, but you are given a "default" orientation that you (may) need to change.

      • From here, choose an edge direction for these ambiguous edges based on logical relationships of cause and effect. It should be fairly obvious which direction the edges should point given the interpretations of the variables involved (though in general, you would need some sound, scientific rationale for orienting an ambiguous edge in a particular direction).

      • The above step will require you use the edge tool (\(X \rightarrow Y\) in the menu) which you select and drag from cause to effect. You will also have to select (by clicking on the edge [obnoxiously, the line, not the arrowhead]) and then delete (hitting the delete key) any suggested edges that you wanted to change.

      • Once you are satisfied (and have a proper DAG structure) hit the Done button.

    4. PM (Parameterized Model): just double click on this to verify the network structure, variable values, and expected CPTs.

    5. Estimator: this module will list the network CPTs and parameters from the structure you learned / provided, plus the data from the first step. (Note: display of the CPTs can be finnicky and it looks like some versions of Tetrad don't show them all -- don't worry, you'll be able to find then using Pomegranate in a future step).

Probabilistic Programming

Now that we have examined the network structure from the step above, it's time to head back to the programmatic side.

For this warmup section, we'll use a few great libraries that are popularly employed in this domain:

  • Pomegranate, a library for Bayesian Inference, which can be installed via the instructions listed on its GitHub page (as simpe as pip install pomegranate):

    Get Pomegranate

    Having trouble with installation? pip install cython before installing Pomegranate.

  • Pandas Data Frames, a library for structuring, reading, and manipulating tabular data like in a .csv file. Getting pandas is as simple as pip install pandas. You can find a tutorial for using its (intuitive) interface here:

    Pandas Tutorial

    For a brisk introduction, focus on the tutorial sections of: Object Creation, Viewing Data, Selection, Operations, Merging, Grouping.


Important Documentation

Once installed, head over to the Pomegranate tutorial on constructing a Bayesian Network, which should be fairly straightforward:

Pomegranate BN Documentation

For our purposes, you'll want to inspect several features of the interface:

  • BayesianNetwork.from_samples to learn a network from a set of samples that we will provide. While we *could* fit the data to the structure you found using Tetrad, we'll just let Pomegranate pick one model from the set of the observationally equivalent ones, which will deliver the same inferences albeit have less intuitive causal structure. You'll want to specify the following parameters:

    • X the data to fit the structure to, which has to be read from the dataset you downloaded above. To read a dataset from a CSV, you'll want to look into the pandas library's read_csv method. Providing this dataset populates the network parameters (learns the CPT values from the data).

    • state_names a list of strings indicating the node names (the order of the columns in the data file, which can be read from it using some handy pandas methods).

    • algorithm='exact' set this named parameter in the BN constructor to give it maximal accuracy. It'll take a bit longer to construct, but will make your work / debugging easier to check.

  • predict_proba parameterized by some evidence, returns a list of each node's CPTs updated by the given evidence. Consult the parameters property of each CPT for the probability values. (Hint: it's easiest just to print out what this method returns and see how to use its components).

    NOTE: Tables listed are in the order of the state_names list you constructed the BN with, and represent \(P(V|e)\) for every variable \(V\) and evidence \(e\) -- these are *not* the CPTs, but rather, the likelihood of each variable under the evidence.

    As such, it is recommended you make a small helper method in the AdAgent to extract the likelihood of any desired variable \(V\) from this distribution format.

Decision Network Programming

Back to the task at hand: you must design an agent that uses a decision network that, when given *some* of the features specified above, returns the best choices for any specified decision variables.

You will do so by completing the skeleton for the AdEngine class that was included in the skeleton.


In particular, you must implement the following methods:

Methods

__init__(data_file, dec_vars, util_map)

Constructor for a new AdEngine constructed atop the given data_file.

Responsible for initializing the Decision Network of the AdEngine.

Parameters are to be specified as follows:

  • :param string data_file: path to csv file containing data on which the network's parameters are to be learned

  • :param list dec_vars: list of string names of variables to be considered decision points for the agent. Example:
    ["Ad1", "Ad2"] for two nodes / data columns named "Ad1" and "Ad2" that represent decision variables.

  • :param dict util_map: discrete, tabular, utility map whose keys are variables in network that are parents of a utility node, and values are dictionaries mapping that variable's values to a utility score, e.g.

      {
    "X": {0: 20, 1: -10}
  }

    ...represents a utility node with single parent X whose value of 0 has a utility score of 20, and value 1 has a utility score of -10

    For simplicity's sake, we'll scale back our ambition and only require that your decision network works for inputs of a *single* chance node parent of the utility node.

    It will be up to you to determine the contributing variables and corresponding utility scores for the Defendotron example!


    The constructor should perform the following:

    1. Construct a new Pomegranate Bayesian Network from the samples given in the data_file

    2. Keep track of the decision, chance, and utility variables through some record-keeping mechanism (noting that variables need not be binary, but that the possible values of any variable can be known in advance through the input dataset -- hint, look up some pandas methods to get all variable vals.)

    3. Initialize any other attributes you may want in the methods that follow

meu(self, evidence)

Given some observed demographic "evidence" about a potential consumer, selects the ad content that maximizes expected utility and returns a dictionary over any decision variables and their best values plus the MEU from making this selection.

:param dict evidence: dict mapping network variables to their observed values, of the format: {"Obs1": val1, "Obs2": val2, ...}

Evidence, for our example, will take the form of observations about a particular user, like their age or political leanings if listed on social media.

:return: 2-Tuple of the format (a*, MEU) where:

  • a*: a dictionary mapping each decision variable to the value that, given the observed evidence, maximizes the EU: {"DecVar1": val1, "DecVar2": val2, ...}

  • MEU: float representing the EU(a* | evidence)

vpi(self, potential_evidence, observed_evidence)

Given some observed demographic "evidence" about a potential consumer, returns the Value of Perfect Information received on the given "potential" evidence being evaluated.

:param string potential_evidence: string representing the variable name of the variable being evaluated

:param dict observed_evidence: dict mapping network variables to their observed values, of the format: {"Obs1": val1, "Obs2": val2, ...}

:return: some float value indicating the VPI

The returned value represents the potential gain in utility from learning about the state of a previously unknown variable, i.e., VPI(potential_evidence | observed_evidence).
